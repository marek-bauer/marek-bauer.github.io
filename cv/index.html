First computer program
October 2008

As a kid, I dreamed of an all-powerful program that could create other computer programs. I never asked my mom for such software, assuming it must be incredibly expensive—after all, it would be the only program you'd ever need! In fifth grade, I stumbled upon my first C++ programming tutorial online. I was amazed that such a powerful tool was free to download, no torrents needed. However, after writing a few small programs, I quickly learned that pointers were challenging, and this "almighty" program felt almost useless in my novice hands. So, I returned to my computer games.

Second attempt at programming
March 2011

Several years ago, after an initial attempt at C++ programming didn't quite pan out, I decided to give programming another shot. Inspired by my hours spent playing the classic Super Mario game on my trusty GameBoy, I set out to create my own platformer with a twist of logic puzzles. During this time, I discovered Java—a language touted for its object-oriented capabilities. I thought this meant I could easily display objects on a screen for my game. Imagine my surprise when I learned that object-oriented programming wasn't quite about visual objects. Eventually, I managed to write a program that encoded text using the Caesar cipher, but my dream of developing a video game was stymied by my limited understanding of Java syntax. The final straw was my struggle to install the Eclipse IDE, leaving me to write code in Windows Notepad.

Technical High School
September 2014 - May 2018

My programming journey truly began at Technical High School "Mechanik", where I pursued an IT technician profile. During the first two years, I delved into the technical intricacies of computers, mastering the art of troubleshooting. Imagine the countless drivers I installed for stubborn network interfaces! Alongside, I tackled programming assignments and puzzles, learning the fundamentals of algorithmic complexity. While my custom version of bubble sort wasn't the most efficient, it remains a proud creation of mine. During my time there, I successfully passed three professional examinations:

  • E12 - Assembly and operation of personal computers and peripheral devices
  • E13 - Designing local computer networks and network administration
  • E14 - Creating web applications and databases, and administering databases

These experiences endowed me with practical skills, from cleaning up my laptop to configuring routers—indispensable for someone who spends a lot of time at the PC. My programming foundation was primarily in C++, where I explored features like operator overloading, classes, pointers, and advanced data structures such as lists and binary trees. I also developed several websites, acquiring most of my CSS knowledge along the way. Overall, my understanding of how computers work was significantly enhanced.

In my third year, I embarked on a one-month internship. As one of the top students, I had the privilege of participating in the Erasmus+ student exchange program. Alongside my peers, I went to Brunswick, Germany, and worked at the PTS Institute. There, I learned the basics of programming microcontrollers and even developed my own clone of the classic Snake game for Arduino.

school
Wrocław University of Science and Technology
October 2018 - January 2022

Half of my education was leading up to this moment. I was thrilled to leave my parents' house and dive into fascinating subjects instead of dry literature. Reality, of course, had its own plans. First, I attended a different university from my high school friends, meaning I had to make new connections. Second, I faced a cultural shock with the formal approach to mathematics. I vividly recall my bewilderment when my first task was to prove that one does not equal zero. Yet, after the initial surprise, I quickly adapted to a system where every theorem demanded proof. To this day, I cherish elegant mathematical proofs.

With the fundamentals of mathematics under my belt, I moved on to more technical classes where programming basics were introduced. I got my hands dirty with C and Java and learned techniques for proving algorithm correctness. Although proving imperative algorithms with loop invariants was a bit tedious compared to induction, it was a valuable learning experience. I also tackled design patterns. While memorizing them all felt like an arduous task, their utility is undeniable.

The fourth semester brought an unexpected turn with the COVID-19 pandemic hitting Poland, pushing us into remote learning. Initially, a two-week break was welcomed, but soon it was back to assignments. During this period, I expanded my knowledge with Prolog, metaheuristic algorithms, topology, and even ventured into less thrilling subjects like computer graphics and web development.

Overall, my university experience was fantastic. I made lasting friendships and developed a deep love for mathematics. The Headmaster's scholarship was a nice bonus that kept my budget intact.

"Lambda" science club
November 2018 - Now

Moving to a new town can be challenging, especially when you're a math enthusiast like me. In search of like-minded friends, I found my way to the local science club. Honestly, the promise of post-club pizza was a significant factor in my decision. Although I wasn't initially captivated by functional programming and formal methods, I joined the "Lambda" science club.

The club's primary goal at first was to teach newcomers, myself included, the COQ proof assistant. Over time, I also delved into Haskell and Rust, which have since become two of my favorite programming languages. It was here that I developed a passion for functional programming, particularly for its strict type systems that help catch errors early in the development process.

Today, our club still meets almost every Wednesday to discuss the latest advancements in type theory.

Quant Scholarship Program
October 2020 - April 2021

During my third year, I had the opportunity to join the prestigious Quant Scholarship Program at Credit Suisse. After successfully passing the entrance exam and interview, I was accepted into the program. The curriculum was divided into two main components. The first focused on financial mathematics, where I gained a deep understanding of the Black-Scholes model, learned to value European and American options, and explored other valuation methods such as the Monte Carlo method. The second component concentrated on the F# language. As part of the program, I developed a simple F# application to value various financial instruments, which I presented as my final project.

Siemens Digital Logistics
March 2021 - October 2022

To complete my studies, I needed to undertake an internship. Since I couldn't find an internship that piqued my interest, I decided to work part-time as a software developer. After applying to several companies, I chose a position at Siemens due to their strong market presence. Following a brief introduction and a health and safety course, I got a taste of what it's like to work as a programmer.

Initially, the vast codebase was overwhelming. However, I quickly learned that starting with smaller tasks was more manageable than diving into large chunks of code. I enjoyed my role at Siemens because it offered a variety of projects, from small to mid-sized. During my year and a half there, I worked on:

  • Developing a native Android application for a logistics center
  • Creating an Ionic application for a postal company
  • Building a Java GWT application to manage a logistics center
  • Developing a tool for Android test automation
  • Creating a Spring application for managing luggage transfer at a small airport

My time at Siemens was a fantastic journey that significantly shaped my skills as a programmer. I cherish the memories of working with a great team. However, when I received a job offer to work with Haskell, I decided to seize that incredible opportunity and move on.

Bachelor of Science dissertation
December 2021

My dissertation explored the challenge of generating true random numbers on a deterministic machine, inspired by my fascination with probability theory. Initially, I considered various approaches, such as developing a game where user behavior would generate entropy or using a public API. However, these methods posed significant testing challenges. Ultimately, I chose to use website response times as my entropy source. Although this approach also had practical limitations, it provided invaluable insights into testing randomness and contributed to my successful graduation. I view this experience as a meaningful achievement.

Master of Science dissertation
September 2023

For my master's thesis, I decided to leverage my knowledge of formal verification and push its boundaries. The topic emerged from a collaboration with friends from the "Lambda" club. They introduced me to a fascinating paper about uniquely representing positive rational numbers using an inductive type, based on the trace of a normalization function. This inspired me to explore other intriguing examples of such normalization functions and to investigate the possibility of transforming normalization functions into inductive types.

As anticipated, deriving a general method from a single example proved challenging. While I discovered a few other examples, a universal transformation method remained elusive. Nevertheless, this project was highly rewarding. I gained significant insights into formal verification in COQ, delved deeply into Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT), and substantially enhanced my understanding of type theory. Thus, I consider this project a success.
